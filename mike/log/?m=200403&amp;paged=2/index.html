Auto Efficiency

Friday, March 26th, 2004

When I read articles like this I just get angry at how dumb the Bush administration’s policies seem. He’s promoting this pie in the sky hydrogen technology that, if implemented, will completely disrupt the auto industry yet he talks like raising fuel economy numbers will destroy the auto industry. He couldn’t appear to be in the oil industry’s pockets more if he tried. (What does hydrogen have to do with oil? Hydrogen is less efficient than gasoline and it takes energy to create it from water. Where does that energy come from? You guessed it, existing utility companies burning oil, natural gas, etc. Overall, hydrogen would make us less energy efficient as a country.)

One word: hybrid. It’s done amazing things with the new Prius and Ford plans on selling an SUV next year which will get 30-40 mpg. It’s a modification of existing engine technology (i.e. works in tandem with a standard gas engine) and has already been proven over the last 5 years by Honda and Toyota.

I say get rid of the special SUV category for fuel economy and call it what it really is: a car. Raise the fuel economy minimum 5 pts in 2009 and another 5 pts in 2014. By then, hybrid technology can be in every new car sold.

Source Control

Thursday, March 25th, 2004

I’ve been using cvs at every single job for the last 5 years. It works and it’s really stable but it’s got some annoying “features”: most notably, it does not version file and directory names so if you just want to change the name of a directory or file, you need to delete the old one (and its contents) and add one with the new name (and its contents). In the process, you lose your revision history.

Enter subversion. I just tried it out today and I like it. Frankly I love the simple zen of cvs on the command line and subversion does nothing to change that - it is trivial to use on the command line and has a laundry list of features above and beyond what cvs provides (including directory versioning). Here’s hoping every future job will use svn instead.

Venturing into (legal!) emusic

Friday, March 19th, 2004

I bought my first downloadable album today. I noticed that Squarepusher had released a new album and knew that Warp Records ran their own music storefront at bleep.com. They were selling Ultravisitor for $10. Not a great price for just bits but I definitely want to support the fact that they provide their music in high-quality MP3 format - no DRM or fascist copy protection involved (unlike iTunes, Napster, et al).

So I forked over the cash and downloaded the bits. The problem was two-fold: no cover art was included and the tracks were simply numbered, not named. Both easily rectified but how hard would it have been to solve both on their end? In the end I would have preferred the price to be closer to $5 but if they fix those two small nits, I would be a satisfied customer.

Update: he’s playing at Antone’s on Wed April 21st. Who wants to go? Death Cab for Cutie is playing at Stubb’s on the 28th too.

Update: I emailed bleep my comments and they replied to note that they are working on both problems. The files should be named properly in the near future while they are also looking into how to provide cover art with the download.
