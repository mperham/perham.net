Microsoft and Vodafone's new services standards

http://www.webservices.org/index.php/article/articleview/1203/1/2/

The executive summary is that Vodafone and MS are partnering to create standards for the use of Web Services over a GSM network. I think this is a poor choice in the long run - tying it to the GSM network just means that the cell carrier will charge the hell out of it and no one will use it. See GPRS for a current example.

There's a better solution: Wi-Fi integration. Every coffee house, hotel, airport, etc has a wireless hotspot these days and they will only get more prevelant. It provides a free network connection (the pay-per-use hotspots will go the way of the dinosaur in a year or two) and from there, additional protocols can be created to solve higher-level problems through standards bodies like the W3C or IETF.

Another thought: consider the rising demand for Voice over IP (VOIP). How long do you think it will take before someone offers an Wi-Fi handset that works without wires like a SIPPhone? It's not going kill off the cell phone but certain market segments might find it cheaper.

October 04, 2003

Websphere

Through my career, I've used pretty much every major Java app server: Weblogic, jboss, tomcat, dynamo, and resin. The only one I haven't used is IBM's Websphere, until now. Webify deploys all of its solutions on Websphere and DB2. And for the most part, Websphere is garbage.

Understand my viewpoint: to me the app server must allow me to quickly write, compile, deploy and test my applications. The middle two steps are almost always controlled by a build process consisting of ant or maven and the design of the app server dictates how easy or difficult application deployment is. The problem is that Webspshere takes the aproach that the developer is an idiot and needs a pretty GUI wizard to deploy the application. This is great for documentation and demos but it is absolutely abysmal for the productivity of a professional developer. Indeed fixing a bug in a piece of code might take 10 seconds to fix and another 10 seconds to compile but with Websphere it takes another minute or two to walk through the stupid deployment wizard in order to test the fix.

The smaller app servers do not have this problem. For the most part they are designed and written by professional developers who understand that a good app server compliments a developer by allowing as much to be automated as possible. With resin, I can fix, compile and deploy an application in seconds rather than minutes. For this reason, the big app servers like Websphere and Weblogic (which pander to the managers and have thousands of superfluous features) will never have the developer mindshare that app servers like jboss and resin do (which do their job with a minimum of fuss).
